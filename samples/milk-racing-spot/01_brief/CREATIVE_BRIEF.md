# Creative Brief — "Fuel the Fastest" (milk × motorsport)

**Deliverable:** Three-scene hero stills for a 30-second sponsorship commercial — a national milk board backing a motorsport team, reframing milk as performance fuel.

**Campaign line:** *Fuel the fastest.*

**Tone:** Cinematic. Prestige car spot meets product beauty. Warm, confident, playful without being cartoonish. The joke is that milk is in a motorsport context — don't over-sell it; let the composition carry the punch.

**Audience:** Viewers who watch racing broadcasts and product sport ads. The ad should read as high-end commercial work first, a milk ad second.

**Avoid:** brand logos (sample stays generic), children, cute mascots, comic-style rendering, heavy text overlays, any reference to real teams or real drivers.

**Palette:** golden-hour amber + warm cream + deep charcoal racing accents. Consistent sunset warmth across all three scenes so they read as one campaign.

## Three-scene arc

1. **Approach** (8 s) — the driver slides into the pit; instead of a fuel hose, a crew member offers up a cold bottle of milk. Relaxed confidence beats spectacle.
2. **The Hero Pour** (10 s) — extreme-macro beauty shot of milk being poured into the driver's bottle. This is the "product moment" — sells the fuel metaphor without words.
3. **Victory** (12 s) — podium, helmet off, triumphant, spraying the bottle like champagne. The milk spray is the campaign's signature image.

Tagline ("FUEL THE FASTEST") and any brand lockups are added in a finishing pass. The hero stills are clean.

## What this sample demonstrates for the bundle

This is a clean-sheet project (no operator-supplied reference mockup, no existing IP). The producer's pre-production moves:

1. Interpret the verbal brief into a three-scene structure with a clear arc.
2. Pick a visual register (photoreal cinematic commercial) and an aesthetic throughline (golden hour + amber palette).
3. Lock compositions in a shot list before generation.
4. Generate against the shot list, one pass per scene.
5. Package the three frames with the brief for operator review.

No D040 / D041 analysis needed on this sample because the operator (this brief) isn't supplying audio or a mockup deck — just a verbal creative ask.
